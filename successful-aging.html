Exploring the Intersection Between Social Life and Successful Aging

NORC reveals the connections between relationships and health.

Studies conducted by NORC have shone an increasingly revelatory light on the important and complex role social interactions play in how well we age. The National Social Life, Health, and Aging Project (NSHAP), conducted in collaboration with a team of University of Chicago faculty investigators with support from the National Institutes of Health (NIH), is a representative, longitudinal survey (3 waves over 15 years) of older adults, including people born between 1920 and 1965 and their partners. Data from NSHAP has shown that age plays almost no role in accounting for differences in older people’s health. A “comprehensive model” of aging that takes into account factors such as psychological well-being, sensory function, mobility, and health behaviors is a better predictor of mortality. NORC also used NSHAP data to provide a report to the American Association of Retired Persons (AARP) Foundation about the prevalence and correlates of loneliness in older Americans. In another study supported by NIH, Activity Space, Social Interaction and Health Trajectories in Later Life, NORC is using innovative data collection methods to explore where and how older adults spend their time, including how social support networks affect and are affected by the mental and physical health of older Chicagoans.
